### LinkedIn Post 2: Zero-Based Design
```
NVIDIA engineers don't iterate. They start from zero.

Before designing anything, they calculate the theoretical physical limit. How fast could this system possibly run? What does physics allow? Then they design backward from that number.

Jensen Huang calls it "speed of light thinking."

Not: "what do competitors ship?"
Not: "what can we improve from v2?"
But: "what are the laws of physics?"

Then they build to that limit.

Every product generation starts from scratch. No legacy code to protect. No "we already have infrastructure for this." Just the question: what would we build if nothing existed before us?

He pairs this with what he calls "systematic forgetting" — deliberately letting go of how previous problems were solved, so past approaches don't anchor current thinking.

This sounds insane for a company with 30 years of hardware legacy and 200+ suppliers. And yet NVIDIA ships a new architecture that makes the previous one obsolete every 2 years.

I've been designing products for 8 years. Most teams I've worked with — including mine — optimize within constraints we never questioned. We inherit assumptions from competitors, from v1, from "how things are done."

Jensen's approach is closer to what designers call "zero-based design." Except most of us just write it on conference slides. He runs a $4T company on it.

The constraint you never questioned might be the one holding back your entire product.

### LinkedIn Post 3: The Four Scaling Laws
```
Jensen Huang broke down AI scaling into 4 distinct phases. This is the clearest framework I've seen for understanding where AI is actually headed.

Phase 1 — Reading (Pre-training)
More data + bigger models = smarter AI. This is the phase we all know. GPT-3 to GPT-4 lived here.

Phase 2 — Augmentation (Post-training)
Human data is running out. So AI generates its own training data synthetically. Ground truth in, refined data out. Quality over quantity.

Phase 3 — Thinking (Test-time compute)
"Thinking is harder than reading." Instead of just pattern-matching, the model reasons, plans, and searches for solutions during inference. This is where Chain-of-thought and o1-style models live.

Phase 4 — Multiplication (Agentic)
One AI spawns sub-agents. Teams of digital workers tackling complex multi-step workflows. Not one model answering questions — a system solving problems.

Here's the punchline: Jensen says data is no longer the bottleneck. Compute is. Because synthetic data is unlimited. Because thinking requires exponentially more compute than reading. Because agents multiply compute demand.

That's why NVIDIA is worth $4 trillion. Not because they make the best chips. Because they understood that every phase of AI scaling increases compute demand, not decreases it.

Every company betting that AI will get "cheaper and more efficient" is betting against all four scaling laws simultaneously.
