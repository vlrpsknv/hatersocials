### L2: Thinking Is Expensive
Hook: LABEL THE UNNAMED
```
There's a concept in AI scaling that changes how I think about product design.

"Thinking is harder than reading."

Reading means processing data. Models got good at this. They scan the internet, absorb patterns, generate text. It's computationally cheap relative to what comes next.

Thinking means reasoning during inference. The model doesn't just pattern-match. It plans, considers alternatives, checks its own logic, searches for better solutions. In real time.

This costs 100x more compute than reading.

That's why the company selling GPUs is worth $4 trillion while the companies building models are racing to cut prices. The bottleneck moved. It's not about who has the best model. It's about who has enough compute to let models actually think.

For product builders, the implication is uncomfortable: the AI features you ship today are "reading" features. Autocomplete. Summarization. Pattern matching.

The next generation of AI features will involve thinking. Planning. Multi-step reasoning. And they'll cost 100x more to run.

If your pricing model assumes AI gets cheaper every year, you might want to reconsider. Every new scaling law makes compute more valuable, not less.
```

### L3: How to Think From Scratch
Hook: CONTRARIAN PROOF
```
Most product teams improve things. The best ones forget things.

There's a practice called "systematic forgetting." You deliberately let go of how you solved a problem before. Not because the solution was bad. Because remembering it anchors your thinking to a local maximum.

Instead of asking "how do we improve v2?" you ask "what would we build if we were starting today with no legacy?"

One hardware company does this for every product generation. Every two years, they ship an architecture that makes the previous one obsolete. Not iterative improvements. Complete rethinks from physics up.

30 years of legacy. 200+ suppliers. 1.3 million components per unit. And they still start from zero each time.

I've been designing products for 8 years. The hardest constraint to question is always the one you inherited without noticing. The framework from v1. The user flow that "works fine." The technical decision someone made 3 years ago that nobody remembers the reason for.

Most teams call starting from scratch "unrealistic." They prefer "continuous improvement." Small tweaks. A/B tests. Incremental gains.

But incremental thinking can't produce something 10x better. Only zero-based thinking can.

What assumption in your current product have you never actually questioned?
```

### L4: The Four Phases of AI
Hook: SPECIFIC NUMBER + FRAMEWORK
```
AI scaling has 4 phases. Most companies are stuck in phase 1.

Phase 1: Reading.
Feed the model more data, make it bigger, it gets smarter. This is GPT-3 to GPT-4. Everyone understands this phase.

Phase 2: Augmentation.
Human data ran out. So the model generates its own training data. Ground truth in, refined synthetic data out. Quality beats quantity. Most frontier labs are here now.

Phase 3: Thinking.
The model reasons during inference. Instead of instant pattern-matching, it plans, searches, and verifies before answering. This costs 100x more compute. This is where o1, o3, and Claude's extended thinking live.

Phase 4: Multiplication.
One AI spawns teams of sub-agents. Each handles a subtask. They coordinate, merge results, and deliver solutions no single model could produce alone. This is where agentic frameworks are heading.

Here's what matters for business strategy:

Every phase increases compute demand. Not decreases it. Synthetic data is unlimited. Thinking burns exponentially more resources. Agents multiply everything.

Companies betting on "AI will get cheaper" are betting against all four phases simultaneously.

The market is pricing this in. The company that understood it earliest is now the most valuable on earth.
```
